Quality: About This Data Set

This data set is on historical inflation rates for the United States from 1914 to 2016, recorded monthly. For the most part, the data set is complete, coherent, and correct. I assessed the data to ensure no values were missing, the numerical values made sense relative to the context, and there were minimized measurement errors.

Is the data complete?

The data set is on historical inflation rates in the United States from 1914 to 2016. The Federal Reserve System, or the central bank of the US, was founded on December 23, 1913 to supervise and regulate financial institutions at a national level. Hence, the first recorded inflation rate was January 1914, which is the first number in my data set. Although the data set claims to provide rates until 2016, it does not provide rates for the months of February till December of 2016. There are 11 missing values at the end. I assessed this in the Wrangler tool. I filled it in using data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics. In terms of inflation data, I believe this data set is fairly complete on it’s own. However, for users who are looking to analyze the economy more carefully during this century, it would be beneficial to add Gross Domestic Product data as well as labor market data. Typically, these three pieces of information are tied together to assess behaviors and trends in the economy.

Is the data coherent?

For the most part, I believe the data is coherent. The numerical values span from -10.7 to 20.7, which is a fairly large distribution of numbers for the inflation rate. What I find most fascinating is that one is able to attribute large historical and political events at times when the inflation rate was extremely volatile (either high or low). For example, one of the largest inflation rates was in December 1918, which was also the end of World War 1. Also, the fact that each numerical value doesn’t jump larger than a percentage point from month to month, makes sense to me. Inflation rates do not drastically change from month to month. In order to help me understand and interpret the data set better, I graphed it into a line chart to hone in on the extreme values. I then verified these “extreme” values with other sources to check whether they were accurate.

Is the data correct?

Initially, I did a scan of the data to see if I could identify outliers or numbers that looked incorrect. The Average column contained a few 0’s, which I immediately knew was incorrect. I also verified my data against the data from the bureau of the labor statistics. Also the fact that each numerical value does not jump larger than a percentage point from month to month makes me believe the data is correct. Biases could have occurred when the data was recorded itself.
